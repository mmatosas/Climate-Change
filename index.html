The climate change crisis

by Marta Matosas Fonolleda

The scientific community has reached a 97% consensus: climate change is happening and is anthropogenic. Moreover, climate change stems from the rise of greenhouse gas concentration in the atmosphere.

Despite this consensus, there seems to be a disconnect between scientists, and the public and the policy makers resulting in a lower response level than what is advised by scientists to avoid serious impact on life on the Earth.

This project aims to visually show the effect of climate change in the climate itself, the impact of human industrial activity, the countries that contribute the most, and their population beliefs on climate change.

Is it really happening?

Introducing the analysis of two key temperature indicators:

1- Rise of the average temperature of the Earth's surface, 1850 - 2019

The variation of the global surface temperature in Celsius (C) is calculated with respect to the mean of the global temperatures from 1850 to 1900, period adopted as an approximation of preindustrial conditions by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.
Three thresholds are marked: 1, 1.5 and 2 degree Celsius. The last two are relevant to the aim of the Paris Agreement of “holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5°C.”

The increment is quickly approaching the 1.5 degrees Celsius (C) threshold. Surpassing it permanently could sink many small island states perpetually. Surpassing the 2C threshold could lead to substantial species extinction, large risks to global food security and inability to live and work outside some areas of the world.

2- Occurrence of temperature anomalies

Temperature anomalies indicate how much warmer or colder it is than normal for a specific place and time. For this analysis, normal is defined as the average over the 30-year period 1951-1980 for that place and time of year in degrees Celsius (C), following NASA definition.

Occurrence of monthly temperature anomalies in 197 countries, 1743 - 2013

Top graph: sum of country temperature anomalies per month and year (Celsius).
Bottom graph: annual average of temperature anomalies per year (Celsius).

The visualization for all countries, regions and seasons clearly indicates a rise of warmer temperatures than normal since the late 1980’s and the trend is stronger since the year 2000, in agreement with the rise of the average temperature of the Earth’s surface.

Different combinations of country, region and season are aligned to the above observation.

Is it caused by humans?

Scientists sustain that human activities have increased the natural concentration of greenhouse gases in our atmosphere , amplifying Earth's natural greenhouse effect. This increase has tipped the Earth's energy budget out of balance, trapping additional heat and raising Earth's average temperature. Out of all the greenhouse gases, carbon dioxide (CO2) has the most impact because it adds to the greenhouse effect in a unique way, staying in the atmosphere much longer.

Evolution of carbon dioxide concentration in the atmosphere, 803,719 BCE - 2018

Time continuous observations of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere began at Mauna Loa Volcanic Observatory in 1958. Prior amounts are based on air bubbles trapped in mile-thick ice cores and other paleoclimate evidence. Negative value years indicate a year Before the Current Era (BCE).

The peaks and valleys mark ice ages (low CO2) and warmer interglacial ages (higher CO2). CO2 was never higher than 300 ppm until 1909, 9 years after the start of the industrial era. The increase from 1909 to 2018, marked by the red line, looks exponential on a geologic time scale. In 2018, it reached 408.5 part per million. This means the increase is greater than 100 parts per million. There is no record of an increase of this magnitude over a period of 109 years.

Let us take a closer look at the period starting shortly before the start of the industrial era to inspect the relationship between the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere and the emissions derived from human activity.

Carbon dioxide concentration in the atmosphere and annual global emissions, 1750 - 2017


Starting in 1850, following the end of the first industrial revolution, both carbon dioxide measures rise, suggesting a direct correlation.

Which countries are contributing the most?

Increases in atmospheric carbon dioxide are responsible for about two-thirds of the total energy imbalance that is causing Earth's temperature to rise. Let us look at the countries' carbon dioxide emissions to identify the top contributors to climate change.

China and the US emit more than three and two times the average, respectively. There are four countries from the European Union (EU-28): Germany, United Kingdom, France and Poland..
Given that carbon dioxide stays in the atmosphere from 300 to 1,000 years, it is also relevant to identify the countries that have contributed the most over time.

What are the public beliefs?

Scientists are prompting the governments to take decisive actions against climate change today. The current plans to reduce carbon dioxide emissions are below the response level defined by the scientific community to avoid a severe negative impact. Policy making is shaped by public belief; let us explore the magnitude of the disconnect between the scientific community and the public from the US and EU-28 based on domestic surveys. Data from China is currently unavailable.

Key findings and conclusions

Climate is happening and is anthropogenic.
The increase of concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere has no precedent. Industrial activity is directly correlated to the increase of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.
The US, the EU-28 and China have the most responsibility over climate change from a carbon dioxide emission perspective.
There is a significant 30% gap between the scientific community and the US public beliefs on climate change. Education and communication activities should be focused on closing this gap to enforce the implementation of more ambitious policies to fight climate change.
Next step for this project is to further explore public beliefs, the top emitters plans and policies to fight climate change and their adherence to those plans.

What is climate change?


Climate change is a long-term change in the average weather patterns that define regional and global climates; it includes - but is not limited to - global warming, the long-term heating of Earth’s climate system.

Weather refers to the way the atmosphere is behaving locally and over a short period of time, from minutes to days. It includes conditions such as temperature and precipitation.

Climate refers to the regional or global average of temperature, humidity and rainfall patterns over a long period of time, usually defined as 30 years.


Data sources

The Earth’s global temperature by month from 1850 to 2019 was obtained from the Met Office Hadley Centre. The data was used to calculate the variation of global temperature with respect to the mean of the global temperatures from 1850 to 1900.
The temperature by country and month from 1743 to June 2013 data was available for download from Kaggle. This data set -“Climate Change: Earth Surface Temperature Data”- was used to calculate the anomalies with respect to the average over the 30-year period 1950-1979 for that place and time of year. The data was further processed to incorporate the geographical region and the season as additional dimensions.
The data relative to the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere from 803719 BCE to 2018 and the annual CO2 emissions by country were downloaded from Our World in Data. The data was further processed to incorporate the geographical region as an additional dimension.
The sources of US and EU beliefs are the Yale Program on Climate Change Communication and the 2019 Eurobarometer survey by the European Commission, respectively. Data relative to the scientific community consensus is based on the paper “Quantifying the consensus on anthropogenic global warming in the scientific literature” by John Cook, Dana Nuccitelli, Sarah A Green, Mark Richardson, Bärbel Winkler, Rob Painting, Robert Way, Peter Jacobs and Andrew Skuce, published in 15 May 2013.
